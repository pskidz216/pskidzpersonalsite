00 / Design foundations

The system the wireframes are already drawn against.

Reviewer note across the deliverable suite — "the stuff seems a little big" — was correct, and measurably so. The wireframes that follow have been redrawn against a corrected type scale (h1 capped at 56px, not 80px) and a corrected hero canvas height (420px, not 560px). The homepage hero is already full-bleed today — image fill matches the cohort. The pages that need image-fill work are inner surfaces (featured fragrance blocks, neighborhood tiles, mobile hero). This section establishes those tokens up front. Every page in Sections 01–04 inherits them.

00.1 / Typography

One step off the line — and the corrected scale.

Bond cites Byredo, Le Labo, MFK, Creed, and Parfums de Marly as spirit animals. Those houses run h1 at 40–56px and h2 at 28–36px. The pre-correction wireframes ran h1 at 68–80px and h2 at 40–52px — one full step above the editorial register the brand sits in. The fix is type-side only; whitespace stays generous.

Why this matters — Bond's own brand spec, re-read
  • — "Large type-to-image ratio" ≠ "make type large." The luxury ratio means small type punctuating large whitespace, the way Byredo renders a 36px h1 inside a 560px hero block. Inflating both sides defeats the rule.
  • — Every clamp() ceiling triggered on every desktop. The pre-correction clamp(48px, 7vw, 80px) caps at 1143px viewport. Standard desktops are 1280px+ → every reader saw the cap, not the fluid mid-range.
  • — Hierarchy was collapsing. Pre-correction h1→h2→h3 ratios were 1.54× and 1.44×. Below 1.5× tiers read as variations of one tier rather than distinct levels. Post-correction: 1.56× and 1.38× — primary tier sharp, sub-tier intentionally tighter.
  • — Whitespace is the luxury signal. Bond's "generous margins, never cramped" rule is preserved. The fix moves type down so whitespace can do its job.

00.5 / Image sizing deep-dive

Big bottles, tight gutters.

Six sites measured at desktop (1440px wide) and mobile (390px wide) across four kinds of pages: homepage, shop-all page, individual product page, and collection / neighborhood page. The "bottles big" position holds — bottles are peer-standard at this register, not a Bond eccentricity. What does run measurably larger than peers is type and chrome: buttons and headlines render at 2× to 2.7× the size of every other house in the set. And the single largest outlier in the entire dataset isn't bottle size at all — it's the gap between product tiles on the shop-all page. Bond's tiles are butted right up against each other; every peer leaves visible breathing room. The question isn't "should the bottles shrink." It's "are the bottles breathing, and is the typography earning its register."

A primer · before the numbers

Six words used throughout this section.

The comparison below uses a handful of layout terms — hero canvas, gutter, card, image-to-canvas ratio. They're standard ecommerce vocabulary, but every team uses them slightly differently. Definitions and a labeled mockup of a typical page below.

01The page
The whole top-to-bottom thing the visitor sees. Bond has three kinds in this analysis: the homepage, the shop-all page (the grid where all fragrances live), and the product page (one specific bottle, e.g. Bleecker Street).
02Hero canvas
The big image area at the top of the page — the "front cover" of a page. On Bond's homepage today, the hero is a wide image rotation. The height of that hero (how much vertical space it takes) and how much of that height the image actually fills are two of the measurements compared below.
03Image-to-canvas ratio
How much of the hero canvas the actual image covers. ~100% fill = the image runs edge-to-edge with no white space around it (cinematic, full-bleed). ~55% fill = the image sits inside a column with a lot of white space around it (boxed-in). Bond's homepage hero today is full-bleed — measured live at 1440px wide × 514px tall, image fills the canvas. Where this metric matters more for Bond is on mobile, where the hero is square (390×390) while peers run 675–844px tall, and on inner pages like category headers and neighborhood tiles where some assets do sit inside narrower columns.
04Product-to-canvas ratio · product page
On a product page (the page for one specific bottle), this is how much of the main photo the bottle takes up vs. the scene around it. Creed's product page: bottle takes ~78% of the photo — classic bottle-on-white. Bond's: bottle takes ~33% — the other ~67% is the neighborhood mural sitting beside it. Same overall photo area, very different compositions.
05PLP card & card height
"PLP" = Product Listing Page = the shop-all grid. Each tile in that grid is a card. Card height = how tall each tile is. Bond's tiles are 40% taller than peer median (654px vs. 481.5px) — the extra height is the stacked name + price block, not the bottle.
06Gutter
The space between tiles in the grid. This is the single largest measured difference between Bond and every peer in the set. Peer median: 22px (visible breathing room between tiles). Bond: 1px (tiles butted edge-to-edge). The shop-all page reads dense and loud because of this — a 30-minute CSS change could fix it.

00.5.2 / PDP composition · editorial-mural vs. bottle-on-white

The PDP is a choice, not a defect.

On the PDP, Bond's bottle shares the canvas with the neighborhood mural — bottle on the left, scene on the right. The bottle is ~33% of the canvas; Creed runs ~78% bottle-on-white. This is the closest spirit to Marly's editorial composition, which leans on florals and mood imagery instead of bottle-isolation. Treat the editorial-mural position as a brand asset to defend, not a number to "fix."

PDP composition · which peer is closest
  • — Closest peer: Parfums de Marly. Both Bond and Marly use the PDP frame to tell a place / mood story (neighborhood mural vs. florals + notes diagram). Bottle isolation is not the spec.
  • — The lone outlier in the other direction: Creed. Bottle-on-white at 78% canvas. Treated as a product hero, not a scene.
  • — The framing: "Bond's PDP is bottle plus story, not bottle alone. That's a brand position. The question isn't whether the bottle should be bigger — it's whether the neighborhood story is doing enough work in that shared canvas."
